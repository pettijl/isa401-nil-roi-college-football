# In-Class Presentation Script

## Slide 1: Title

**Talent, NIL, and Results: Are College Football Programs Converting Player Investment Into Wins?**

What to say:

> Our project looks at college football in the NIL era. The main idea is that roster building is now more financially driven than ever. We wanted to know which programs appear to be getting the best on-field return from player investment.

## Slide 2: Business Problem

What to say:

> NIL has created a new problem for athletic departments, donors, and collectives. There is more money flowing toward players, but it is not always clear whether that money actually leads to better results. If a donor is helping fund a program, the big question is where that money should go and whether the program is getting a return from that investment.

## Slide 3: Research Question

What to say:

> Our research question was: in the NIL era, which college football programs are generating the best on-field return from player investment, and does success appear to come more from star-player value, quarterback value, or broader roster talent?

## Slide 4: Data Sources

What to say:

> We used three main types of data. First, we used public On3 NIL valuation data as a proxy for player market value. Second, we used CollegeFootballData for wins, losses, team talent, recruiting, SRS ratings, and team stats. Third, we used athletics finance data from Knight-Newhouse or EADA as a broader proxy for athletic department resources.

Add this important limitation:

> Actual NIL payments are private, so our project does not claim to know exact NIL spending. We are using public valuation and finance proxies to study the relationship between player resources and football performance.

## Slide 5: NIL Market Landscape

Visual: Top teams by total public NIL valuation.

What to say:

> This first visual shows where public NIL value appears to be concentrated. These teams are not necessarily spending the most in exact dollars, but they have the highest visible player-market value based on the players included in the public NIL valuation data.

## Slide 6: NIL Value vs. Winning

Visual: Scatterplot of total public NIL value vs. win percentage.

What to say:

> This is one of the main visuals. The x-axis shows public NIL value, and the y-axis shows win percentage. If NIL value perfectly translated into success, the highest-value teams would all be near the top. But the pattern is more mixed, which suggests that resources matter, but they do not guarantee winning by themselves.

## Slide 7: Best NIL ROI Programs

Visual: Top teams by NIL ROI score.

What to say:

> These are the teams that performed better than expected based on the variables in our model. These programs may not have the highest NIL value overall, but they appear to be converting their available resources into wins efficiently.

## Slide 8: Lowest NIL ROI Programs

Visual: Bottom teams by NIL ROI score.

What to say:

> These are the teams that fell short of expectations. This does not automatically mean NIL money was wasted, but it shows a gap between visible player value, program resources, and actual results.

## Slide 9: Star Spending vs. Balanced Roster Value

Visual: NIL concentration or QB NIL share vs. win percentage.

What to say:

> We also looked at whether value is concentrated in a few players or spread across the roster. This matters because donors and collectives have to decide whether money is better spent on a few stars, a quarterback, or broader roster depth.

## Slide 10: Conference Comparison

Visual: Conference summary table or heat map.

What to say:

> At the conference level, we compared average NIL value, talent, win percentage, and ROI. This helps add context because winning in one conference is not the same as winning in another, and some conferences have much higher resource levels than others.

## Slide 11: Final Takeaways

What to say:

> Our main takeaway is that NIL and player-market value matter, but they do not guarantee success. The best programs are not just the ones with the most visible player value. They are the ones that turn that value into wins. For donors and athletic departments, the best use of NIL money may be targeted roster building instead of simply chasing the most expensive or highest-profile players.

Final line:

> In short, college football is now a return-on-investment problem, and this project helps identify which programs appear to be getting the best return.
